This audit runs before discovery. It scores what the market perceives before the founder tells us anything. Seven dimensions. Scored 1–7.

Trust Factors at 1.8 is the most surprising. The material exists: VA background, Marine Corps operating system, climbing gym origin story. None of it is in the brand. This is not a trust deficit in the product — it is a visibility problem in how the brand has been communicating.
Which gap is structural (hard to fix fast) vs. executional (fixable in weeks)?
Consistency (1.8) is executional — it can close in weeks with a clear visual standard and a working copy stack. Trust Factors (1.8) is partially structural: the case study does not exist yet, so the proof gap cannot fully close until Phase 1 completes. Everything else is executional.

Messaging problem. The low scores cluster around credibility transfer: strong internal material, missing from brand outputs. The product is real. The team is credible. The brand output has not caught up yet — and that is the most fixable kind of gap.

Phase 01 / Identity
Who they are.
Foundation questions. What the brand is built on: the thing you can't fake.
Q1 / Identity
What does your company do, and who does it serve?
Client Input ✎
GroeFit provides AI-powered retention analytics to fitness facilities. We use computer vision and machine learning to track member behavior patterns — workout frequency, equipment usage, and engagement signals — then surface insights that help gym operators reduce churn before it happens. We serve mid-to-large fitness facilities: big box gyms, boutique studios, and multi-location operators.
Strategist's Read ✎
He leads with mechanism, but customers buy the outcome. The real sentence is buried at the end of this answer: we help gyms keep the members they're already losing. Computer vision is the delivery mechanism. That distinction matters in every sales conversation, because the product story and the brand story need to run in sequence and right now they're running as one.
⚠ Watch Signal: Lead with the outcome for the client. The mechanism is proof, not position.
Q2 / Identity
What's the origin story? Why did you start this?
Client Input ✎
I started GroeFit after struggling with my own fitness consistency. I kept joining gyms and falling off. I had the data background to wonder — why don't gyms know I'm about to churn before I do? A friend who managed a climbing gym told me they lose 40% of members in the first 90 days and have almost no visibility into why. I met my CTO at a climbing gym and we built the first prototype on our phones.
Strategist's Read ✎
This is a better origin story than he knows he's telling. He was solving his own problem and found a bigger one. He found his co-founder while literally doing the thing the product is about. The serendipity is real, the founder-market fit is real, and the mythos is already written. The job is to pull it forward rather than bury it inside a feature description.
↑ Strong Signal: Use the climbing gym. The co-founder meeting while doing the thing is gold. Build the scene.
Q3 / Identity
Why are you uniquely positioned to do this?
Client Input ✎
I'm a Marine Corps veteran with a background in data science. After the Corps I ran a data science bootcamp until COVID shut it down. I kept going with the skills and consulting work. My co-founder has a master's in computer science and 8 years in ML systems engineering. We have the rare combination of technical depth and real operator experience — people who've run things under pressure, not just built things in a lab.
Strategist's Read ✎
The Marine Corps runs on a specific operating system: know yourself, seek self-improvement, be technically and tactically proficient. He started a bootcamp to fill his own gap, the bootcamp shut down, and he kept going. That sequence tells the trust story more directly than any credential document. The VA health integration is believable because of it. The product roadmap is credible because of it.
↑ Strong Signal: Reframe Marine Corps through the operating system lens. The bootcamp-to-consulting sequence shows accountability.
Q4 / Identity
What personal values drive how you run this company?
Client Input ✎
Trust above all. We won't deploy something we don't believe in. Stewardship of data — member data is a responsibility, not a resource to extract. Competitive drive — I genuinely hate losing, and I think that matters. Going the extra mile because that's what creates loyalty. Connectedness — fitness is a community thing, not an individual thing. We believe in jobs: real economic opportunity, not just metrics. Success is never about money for me. Everyone should have a voice.
Strategist's Read ✎
Three clusters landed: relationship values (trust, stewardship, connectedness), performance values (competitive drive, going the extra mile), social values (jobs, community, everyone having a voice). A brand needs all three visible because each one is doing different work. Relationship values earn the gym's trust. Performance values make the technical ambition credible. Social values give the long arc meaning. "Success is never about money" is a rare claim. It earns its weight only when you can prove it.
↑ Strong Signal: Three distinct value clusters, each doing different work. Map them to brand expression accordingly.
Q5 / Identity
Tell me about your founding team.
Client Input ✎
CEO: me — vision, sales, business development, relationship building. CTO: 8 years ML engineering, built complex health data systems as a VA subcontractor (federal-grade security and compliance). COO: Background in B2B SaaS go-to-market, has scaled sales motions at three companies. We're a team of three and we cover the full stack: vision, tech, and revenue.
Strategist's Read ✎
The VA subcontractor work is the most underused asset in this entire intake. Complex health data integration for a federal contractor is the answer to every enterprise security objection a COO will raise before she's ready to sponsor the conversation upstairs. Federal-grade compliance is a higher bar than any gym will ever set. That goes in front of every first conversation. The team is complete for this stage: vision and relationships, technical depth, B2B go-to-market. That's the founding structure that makes the near-term case.
↑ Strong Signal: CTO's federal health data background pre-answers the security objection. Lead with it.
Phase 02 / Expression
How the brand shows up.
Voice, aesthetic, values in motion, archetype. The brand as a behavioral standard.
Q6 / Expression
How do you want customers to feel after interacting with your brand?
Client Input ✎
Understood. Like we actually know what's happening in their gym and we're on their side. Informed — not overwhelmed, but genuinely smarter about what to do next. And eventually, for the consumer side, I want people to feel like they belong to something. Like this is a fitness community that goes beyond their individual gym.
Strategist's Read ✎
Two emotional targets are colliding here. The B2B buyer wants operational relief and uncommon insight. The B2B2C vision wants belonging and a fitness community that transcends tribalism. Both are real. Both matter. They belong in different conversations at different stages of the arc. The gym COO who signs the first contract does not buy belonging. She buys proof and a reduction in staff wasted effort. Community is the consumer layer, and it comes later. Keep them separate until the brand has earned the right to hold both.
↔ Calibrate Signal: Separate B2B emotional target (insight, relief) from B2B2C vision (belonging). Different conversations.
Q7 / Expression
Describe your communication style. How do you naturally talk about your work?
Client Input ✎
Direct. Sometimes to the point of being abrasive. I don't have a lot of patience for vagueness. I also care a lot about follow-through — if I say something, I mean it and I do it. I think the brand should have that same energy: confident, data-informed, and accountable. I've learned to soften the edges a bit, especially in sales. When I get something wrong I own it fast and fix it.
Strategist's Read ✎
Direct to the point of abrasive is more useful information than just direct. The brand voice should carry the speed and conviction without the drop-it energy. The recovery instinct is the key signal: own the mistake, name what happened, commit to the fix. That is stewardship in practice. The tone that earns trust with a COO is confident without being conclusory. It says "I already know what's happening in your gym" rather than "let me explain how our software works."
↔ Calibrate Signal: "Own it fast and fix it" is a brand behavior, not just a founder trait. Build it into the standards.
Q8 / Expression
What's the one thing you believe more deeply than anything else about your market?
Client Input ✎
Gyms are flying blind. They have members showing them signals every single day — workout patterns, attendance gaps, equipment preferences — and they're not reading any of it. The information is already there. The accountability is not. If you give someone accurate intelligence and they act on it, the outcome changes. That's not a hypothesis. We've seen it in early testing.
Strategist's Read ✎
The load-bearing belief is trusted intelligence: accountability for the insight you give someone who then acts on it. That is a harder claim than data precision and a more differentiated one than feature accuracy. It becomes the standard every piece of content has to meet before it scales. If something does not earn this belief first, nothing else it contains will land.
↑ Strong Signal: "Trusted intelligence" is the brand's north star. Every deliverable runs against this test.
Q9 / Expression
What will you always do? What will you never do?
Client Input ✎
We will always prove it. If we're making a claim, we have data behind it. We'll always be transparent with how we use member data. We'll always prioritize the gym's relationship with the member over our own data goals. We'll never position ourselves against the gym member — they are not the product, they're the person we're ultimately trying to serve. We'll never oversell what the product can do right now.
Strategist's Read ✎
"Never position against the gym member" is a strategic guardrail most analytics companies do not make explicit. It is the rule that keeps this brand viable when the business model extends to direct-to-consumer. The member is both the product and the eventual customer, which is the architecture of the long arc rather than a conflict to manage. "Always prove it" is the operational version of the load-bearing belief. It cascades into every deliverable, pitch, and case study the brand produces.
↑ Strong Signal: Two operating standards that become brand filters. These belong in the Decision System.

Working Notes
What would push this brand toward the wrong end of any of these spectrums — and what would that look like in execution?
• Over-correcting on warmth to close a deal is how the Tone slides from Direct to Conversational — and once it does, the data-intelligence signal goes with it • The Belief-Led position at 60% is the one clients most often want to soften. The moment you drop to 50/50, you're just another data company with a nice logo • Design is where the spectrums break first. Lean Minimal + Conceptual is hard to brief. Most designers default to safe.
Which spectrum placement is most counterintuitive for this category — and why is that actually right?
• Direct tone at 35% (vs. Conversational). Fitness tech trends warmer. GroeFit's credibility depends on not following that pull • Belief-Led at 60% for a B2B data product. Most B2B data brands go Function-Led. The conviction that intelligence is the unlock is exactly what makes the category claim defensible • Lean Minimal for a product that processes a lot of data — competitors overcommunicate complexity. GroeFit's restraint signals confidence.
Where does the aesthetic direction diverge from what competitors are doing, and is that divergence defensible?
• Competitors: busy dashboards, data visualization everywhere, ‘you are smart for using us’ aesthetic. This direction is stripped and confident instead • The risk: too minimal reads as not having a product yet. The balance point is visible intelligence underneath clean surfaces • The comp to earn: Calm (wellness tech) × Palantir (serious data infrastructure). Not either fully — somewhere between them.
